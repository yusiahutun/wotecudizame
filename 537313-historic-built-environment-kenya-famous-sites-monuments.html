Historic built environment in Kenya: 20 famous sites and monuments

Publish date: 2024-08-25

Kenya has an illustrious history full of hope and some severely dark times that its people continue to reckon with. The historic built environment in Kenya, from museums to monuments, brings Kenyan history to life and educates people on the founding principles and legacy of the country.

TABLE OF CONTENTS

Kenya is home to various fascinating historic sites, from Fort Jesus, built by the Portuguese, to the iconic All Saints Cathedral. Traversing the country's origin up to the 21st century, exploring Kenya’s history is an intriguing and worthwhile pursuit.

Historic built environment in Kenya

While Kenya is famous for its stunning natural attractions and wildlife, there are several exciting monuments and landmarks to explore. The country has sites of cultural and historical importance that are still worth visiting today. Here is a list of historic built environment in Kenya.

1. Lamu Old Town

Lamu Old Town is the best-preserved and oldest Swahili settlement in East Africa, keeping its traditional functions. Built using mangrove timber and coral stones, the beautiful town is characterised by simple structures enriched by features like verandas, an inner courtyard and carved wooden doors.

Since the 19th century, Lamu has hosted major religious festivals and is an important centre for studying Swahili and Islamic cultures.

2. Fort Jesus

Fort Jesus overlooks the entrance to Mombasa's Old Port and is one of the most famous historic sites in Kenya. After several Turkish raids, the Portuguese built this architectural masterpiece in the 16th century.

It was under Portuguese rule for a century and helped make Mombasa a major trading centre. The fort is a testimony to the first successful attempt of the Westerners to rule the Indian Ocean trade routes – which until then were under Eastern Influence.

While Fort Jesus isn’t wholly intact, its habitable part now houses a museum featuring traded pots and ceramics.

3. Bomas of Kenya

The Bomas of Kenya was established in 1971 by the Kenyan government to preserve, maintain and promote the rich and diverse cultural values of various Kenyan ethnic groups. It features one of the largest auditoriums in Africa, with a capacity of 3,000. Tourists get to witness approximately 50 dances performed by different ethnic communities.

4. Mijikenda Kaya Forests

The sacred Mijikenda Kaya Forests consists of 11 separate forest sites. They are the remains of numerous fortified villages known as Kayas. In the 1940s people abandoned these villages created in the 16th century and are now considered sacred sites, protected by the councils of elders.

5. All Saints Cathedral

The All Saints Cathedral, one of the oldest buildings in Kenya, was conceptualised in 1914 at a meeting led by Governor Henry Conway. Temple Lushington Moore, a Grand Master among the Freemasons, designed the cathedral, and until World War II, only the Chancel Arch had been built from the entire structure.

After an appeal to continue building the cathedral, it was finally completed in 1952, and on March 21st that year, Bishop Crabbe consecrated it.

6. Gedi Ruins

The historic Gedi Town is located 94 km north of Mombasa Town. It was a small and beautiful Swahili town built in the 15th century entirely from stones and rocks. In 1927, Gedi Town, which occupies 44 hectares, was declared a historic monument, and through great preservation, most of the original foundations can still be seen today.

7. Kenya National Archives

The Kenya National Archives, situated at the edge of Nairobi CBD, was established in 1965. Its primary role is to preserve valuable public records and make them accessible to present and future generations.

The building also houses the Murumbi Gallery, which has artefacts from the 19th century. It’s currently the largest Pan-African art gallery in Africa and has ancient art collections from different African regions and communities.

8. Vasco da Gama pillar

The Vasco da Gama pillar, named after the Portuguese who built it, is one of the oldest European monuments in Africa, having been built in 1498. It is about 120km from Mombasa town next to the Indian Ocean in Malindi.

9. Jumba la Mtwana Ruins

Jumba la Mtwana, in English, "the large house of the slave," is situated in Kilifi along the Kenyan coast. Within this area, a tomb, four houses and four mosques have survived in recognisable condition. It dates back to the 14th century.

10. Lamu Fort

The Lamu Fort, built between 1813 and 1821 by Omanis, protected Omani interests along the East African coast, but the town later lost its economic importance. During the British colonial rule and after Kenya's independence, Lamu Fort was used as a prison. Today, it houses a library and an environmental museum, often used for community events.

11. Dedan Kimathi statue

During the British Colonial rule, Dedan Kimathi (1920-1957) was the senior spiritual leader of the Kikuyu rebels involved in the Mau Mau uprising.

Under the administration of President Kibaki, a 2.1-meter bronze statue named "Freedom Fighters Dedan Kimathi" was installed on a graphite plinth as a tribute to his legacy. In the statue, Kimathi is depicted wearing iconic military attire, clutching a dagger in his left hand and a rifle in his right, symbolizing the final weapons he possessed before his capture.

12. Tom Mboya monument

Located on Moi Avenue in Nairobi, Kenya, the Tom Mboya monument was established in 2011 to commemorate the intelligent Kenyan politician Tom Mboya, who was tragically assassinated in 1969. It is located approximately 20 meters from where he was shot. The emotion-packed monument cost the Kenyan government Ksh. 20,000,000.

13. Nairobi Gallery

Nairobi Gallery is situated at the Uhuru Highway and Kenyatta Avenue roundabout next to Nyayo House. The building (a great example of Victorian architecture) is deeply connected to the development and history of Nairobi and its people.

Built in 1913, the Nairobi Gallery was fondly referred to by the British settlers as "Hatches, Matches and Dispatches" because of the many births, marriages and deaths recorded there.

14. Lamu Museums

Lamu Museums, established in 1960, consists of five museums in the Lamu Old Town. They feature artefacts from the 1900s, showcasing the different Swahili cultural heritage facets. These artefacts include furniture, jewellery, clothing, dhow materials, amulets, and manuscripts.

15. War memorial statue

While Kenya was under the British at the time, Historians argue that Kenyans were forced to participate in a war they had nothing to do with. The war memorial statue, sculpted in 1924 and erected on-site in 1928, is a homage to Kenyans who fought alongside the British soldiers in World War I. It stands in the city centre, Kenyatta Avenue.

16. Nairobi Railway Museum

The Nairobi Railway Museum was opened in 1971. Owned by Kenya Railways, it's the only Railway Museum in East and Central Africa. You'll be impressed by the museum's fascinating collection of historic photographs, maps, furniture, models and artefacts.

17. Uhuru Gardens Memorial Park

The Uhuru Gardens Memorial Park is located along Lang'ata Road, just 15 minutes from Nairobi CBD. "Uhuru" is a Swahili word meaning freedom.

The Uhuru Gardens Memorial Park was officially declared a national monument in 1966 because of its historic importance. It is Kenya's birthplace and where the first Kenyan flag was raised, thus marking the first year of independence on December 12, 1963.

18. Nairobi National Museum

The Nairobi National Museum is located on Museum Hill. It showcases Kenya's beautiful and rich heritage with four distinct themes representing Kenya's history, culture, nature and contemporary art.

Visitors can explore additional attractions within the compound, such as botanical gardens and a snake park. Additionally, an auditorium intermittently hosts film screenings, concerts, and various events, providing diverse entertainment options for guests to enjoy.

19. Nairobi War Cemetery

The Nairobi War Cemetery is situated about 10km west of the city centre on Ngong Road. The cemetery was opened in 1941 and is the largest war cemetery in East Africa. It houses the graves of casualties from World War II, the East African campaign, the Battle of Madagascar, etc. The cemetery is open to the public and is a popular photography site.

20. Jomo Kenyatta Mausoleum

Located at the Parliament Buildings, Nairobi, the Jomo Kenyatta Mausoleum is among the few architectural masterpieces open to the public. It is the final resting place of the first Kenyan prime minister and president, Mzee Jomo Kenyatta. The mausoleum attracts top dignitaries and visiting heads of state who come to pay homage to the former president.

What is the importance of historic built environment in Kenya?

The historic built environment in Kenya are primary sources, just like diaries, documents and letters. They tell people about their origin and Kenya's complex and intertwined shared narrative.

Wrapping up

The historic built environment in Kenya, i.e. museums and monuments, helps locals and tourists to take a step back in time to experience every bit of the rich history that shaped the nation into what it is today. And amazingly, Kenya has a lot of incredible historic sites to visit.

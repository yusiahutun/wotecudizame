what you need to know

Publish date: 2024-03-26

The rise and rise of cosmetic procedures within the mainstream doesn't necessarily equate to more and more. One of the tweakments trending on social media and within celebrity circles currently is the breaking down of dermal filler – to reverse the effects of having it in the first place.

With the likes of famous faces such as Courtney Cox talking candidly about her experience of having filler dissolved, and the speculation surrounding the shrinking pout of original lip filler poster girl, Kylie Jenner, others are following suit (for evidence see the TikTok videos tagged #dissolvingfiller racking up 28.8M views, chasing the 73.3M views tagged #lipdissolving).

"It represents that people are perhaps now reassessing their beauty ideals; being more respectful of their unique beauty and anatomy," respected aesthetic practitioner Dr Wassim Taktouk tells us. "People are more in-tune with picking up on unnatural appearances, like overfilled lips and unnatural jaw lines, and so are wanting to pare it back down." That's not to say there's a return to natural in its literal sense. "The use of filler hasn’t dropped, it’s the overuse that is being corrected."

Jemal Countess/GA//Getty Images

Dissolving filler feels particularly pertinent when talking lips. As cosmetic doctor Dr Saleena Zimri tells us, "the increase in the media around dissolving filler can heavily influence the under 35 age group and it has translated to in-clinic requests". She explains that "a lot of women have had lip filler for several years now and alongside aesthetic trends their personal style might have changed; now the lips are looking overfilled and so they want to restart". Shifting focus from volume to definition, "patients are dissolving old filler and looking for a more natural look that's in proportion while defined".

Injectors' techniques have advanced too, as have the fillers they are working with. "We are more in tune with different ethnicities and different age groups; we understand that it is not a one-size-fits-all approach," offers Dr Taktouk. "For example, Teoxane has five different fillers that you can use for lips, because every lip is unique and needs to be treated as such. The beauty ideals and proportions that were once taught to practitioners as gospel are no longer relevant – you can’t apply mathematical ratios to every face." But whether you're looking to start afresh with filler, or stop with it altogether, it's important to know what dissolving filler really involves and whether it's right for you.

What dissolving filler involves

Dr Christine Hall, who works with Dr Taktouk at the Taktouk Clinic, explains that not all filler is dissolvable. "It’s only possible with fillers made of hyaluronic acid," she explains. "We dissolve them with an enzyme hyaluronidase, which is a prescription-only medicine that should only be used by medical practitioners."

"Some people are allergic to hyaluronidase so we need to do a test injection first," she adds. Indeed, an initial assessment is imperative, feels Dr Taktouk. "Ideally the practitioner who did the treatment should dissolve it, but sometimes the patients can lose confidence in the practitioner who did the treatment, so we do see a lot of patients coming to see us for the dissolving of other practitioners work."

The treatment itself involves injections into the areas where you’ve had dermal filler. "The enzyme breaks down hyaluronic acid and it is then excreted by the body," explains Dr Zimri. But it’s also important to note that the hyaluronidase can’t distinguish between hyaluronic acid in the filler, and the hyaluronic acid that is naturally occurring in your skin, "so you do lose some of your own hyaluronic acid to a certain extent" cautions Dr Hall.

Another thing to bear in mind, adds Dr Taktouk, "is you can get bruising and swelling from the treatment with hyaluronidase, which can be a little uncomfortable for some patients".

In terms of outcome, though, "you actually see an immediate result as it starts to dissolve the filler straight away and actually continues to do so for a few days after the treatment before it then stops working," Dr Hall adds.

How to know if dissolving filler is right for you

Besides knowing exactly which filler has been used on you – should you be seeing a different practitioner to have it dissolved – you need to be over the age of 18 and without contraindications (such as pregnancy).

In general, you are a suitable candidate if you "want to remove filler that you don’t like anymore, or have complications associated with filler such as lumps and bumps or migration of filler," confirms Dr Zimri.

Your options after having filler dissolved

Of course, the easiest option after having filler dissolved is to do nothing, and reacquaint yourself with your unfilled face. Alternatively "you could consider refilling the area with a gentler approach," says Dr Taktouk, "and maybe a better choice of product such as Teoxane, with its patented Resilient Hyaluronic Acid technology that gives more natural stretchability, moving with tissues to look a bit more seamless and sophisticated where injected".

Dr Zimri admits that "99 per cent of the time, when a patient has lip filler dissolved, we have to refill". She agrees that the evolution of fillers means that they can now move more with one's natural expressions, stopping the results looking stiff as they might have done in the past (her favourite filler for lips is the RHA KISS from Teoxane RHA's range).

If you do go down the refill route, know that it can't be achieved immediately. "We advise one-to-two weeks between dissolving and refill to ensure the enzyme is no longer working and that any swelling has settled," she adds.

Either way, "do your research" says Dr Hall. As the industry is highly unregulated always see medical practitioners who have extensive experience in dissolving filler as much as administering it.

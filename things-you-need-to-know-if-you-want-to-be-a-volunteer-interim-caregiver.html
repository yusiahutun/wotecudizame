9 Things to Know About Volunteering to Be an Interim Caregiver

Publish date: 2024-08-03

Last week we ran a story about the desperate need for volunteer interim caregivers who are willing to welcome newborn babies into their families while adoption plans are being considered or made—and you all went ga-ga! (Us too.) If you're one of the many who are now considering signing up to volunteer, here are the basics on becoming an interim caregiver:

1. Who do I contact?

"Prospective volunteers are encouraged to visit our website to review the detailed information before applying," says Katie Foley, Associate Director of Outreach at Spence-Chapin Services to Families & Children. "We are currently looking for volunteers living within 100 miles of New York City, including New Jersey and Long Island. (If you don't live in this area, don't worry. Foley says to contact a foster care or adoption organization in your community to inquire about local volunteer opportunities. Click here for adoption agency information by state.)

2. What is the screening process like?

"Each volunteer family is screened and certified by Spence-Chapin," says Foley. "All adult household members will [go through] complete background checks and child abuse clearances, and need to submit forms about their medical health, provide references, interview with our staff, and complete a home visit."

3. How long before I can bring a baby to my home?

"First, the staff will provide training on newborn care and roles and expectations," says Foley, a licensed social worker. She says due to the coordination of paperwork and necessary meetings, it can take up to two months to become an active volunteer. But it's worth the wait!

4. What does an interim caregiver actually do?

You're not just signing up to provide cuddles. "Our program requires interim care families to provide 24/7 care to a newborn in their home," says Foley. "A baby may remain in care for several days or several weeks." Translation: this isn't a job or a gig — it's your life! Foley tells CountryLiving.com that Spence-Chapin volunteers are required to "provide a safe and nurturing family atmosphere of acceptance, kindness, and understanding." In addition to providing each infant with the attention and care that he or she needs to thrive, volunteers are also asked to promote normal development and attachment, document milestones by taking pictures and write down day-to-day observations. Interim care providers must also handle all well-baby visits and medical appointments, and attend Spence-Chapin's required training sessions. (Sometimes birth parents will want to visit the baby, so you'll have to set those meetings up, too.)

 5. But not all programs require the baby to live in your home—so do your research to find the right fit for your schedule.

"Our [interim care] program is on-site at the Evanston, Illinois location," says Joan Jaeger, who handles Outreach and Communications for The Cradle and AdoptionLearningPartners.org. The Cradle's on-site nursery operates 24 hours a day, 7 days per week. "Cuddlers are volunteers who assist in the care of the babies, like feeding, holding, comforting and playing," says Jaeger. You can learn more about the requirements of becoming a "cuddler" at The Cradle here.

6. How do I pay for the baby's care?

At Spence-Chapin, there are no fees involved with being an interim caregiver. "We pay 100 percent of all the expenses [to care for the newborn]," said Adam Cotumaccio, president of interim care provider Spence-Chapin. "We have a full clinic here where we have pediatricians. We pay for the transportation costs, diapers, even the car seats, as sometimes the volunteer may not have all of the equipment." Take note that this may not be the case with all interim care programs, so ask the agency you choose to work with about any expenses.

7. Be honest with yourself about the fact that you may become emotionally attached.

Foley says attachment is an expected and natural part of caring for infants. "Volunteers share that they find fulfillment and satisfaction in providing a loving, temporary home for a baby in need — and during a time when biological parents are making one of the most important decisions of their life." But this is not a foster care program, cautions Foley. "This is not a path to adoptive parenting," she says. "Biological parents retain their legal rights while the baby is in care and are encouraged to visit their baby."

8. What is a day in the life of an interim caregiver like?

"I am not a 'cuddler' and this is not a 'job,'" says Ann Lapin, 39, from Bronx, New York. "I parent these babies 24 hours a day—feeding them, diapering them, swaddling them, taking them to their doctors' visits. I make sure I have enough of the proper formula, bottles and diapers, and correct clothes for the season. We are their parents, and they are members of our families, if only for a few weeks." Lapin wants people considering becoming an interim caregiver to know that this is a family affair, just like bringing your own newborn home from the hospital.

9. Being an interim caregiver can be incredibly rewarding.

"My favorite part of being an interim boarding care provider is being a part of the permanent story of these families," says Lapin. "From the day the babies are placed in my care, I look forward to the day I hand them to their parents so they can start their lives together."
